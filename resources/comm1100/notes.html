Economics

Overview

  • Economics is a social science.
  • In economics, there are two main tools:
    • Models (often mathematical) that highlight essential features of reality relevant to the question at hand.
    • Empirical tools that use statistical and experimental features to estimate casual relationships using societal data.

Sunk and opportunity costs, marginal benefits and costs.

Consider buying a concert ticket for $50, and then having the decision to take the train to the concert for $3 or just stay home; if you value the concert experience as $55 dollars; what should you do?

You should go to the concert.

Here, $50 is the sunk cost; it's money that's already been invested into the decision and can't be retrieved. Thus, the opportunity cost is $47, and the cost of the ticket is $3, so we should go to the concert!

Here, $50 is our marginal cost, whereas $70 is our marginal benefit; thus our marginal profit is $20.

Simply:

  • Sunk cost: The sunk cost are costs when an opportunity arises, that are already paid for.
  • Opportunity cost: The opportunity cost is the loss of gain from the next best opportunity besides the opportunity we are choosing.

Decision making principles

The cost-benefit principle

  • Evaluate the full set of costs and benefits associated with the choice
  • Pursuie the choice only if the benefits are at least as great as the costs.

The opportunity cost principle

  • The true cost of something is the cost of the next best alternative; by this way, we can consider what we are losing when making a decision
  • When making a decision, consider the trade-offs you are making.

The marginal principle

  • Decisions about quantities are best made incrementally.
  • You should break "how many" questions into marginal decisions (increments), weighing the benefits of each new block.
  • For example, how much more profit do we get by making 1 more shoe per month?

The interdependence principle

  • Everything is connected.
  • The best choice is the choice that considers all the factors in your decision; including the actions of other entities.
  • Act accordingly to your predictions of how other competitors and engagers into the market will react.

Market; supply and demand

Comparative advantage

Example

Imagine two cafe owners, Paul and Cassie. In an hour, Paul can create one pastry and one coffee. Cassie can create 2 pasties and 4 coffees. Note that they cannot do both at the same time.

Note that when Paul makes a pastry, his opportunity cost is 1 coffee. When Cassie makes a pastry, her opportunity cost is 2 coffees. For making a coffee, Paul's opportunity cost is 1 pastry, and Cassie's opportunity cost is half a pastry.

It's clear that for Paul, (if these products are equally priced), that it doesn't matter what he makes - whereas for Cassie, she is much more efficient at making coffee.

Definition

Thus, comparative advantage considers the advantage businesses or people withing businesses have over eachother in the manafacturing of goods and the sale of goods. It explains why businesses specialise into specific areas.

Supply and demand

Before we consider the supply and demand model, we must assume:

  • The consumer is a price taker; they must take the market price.
  • The consumer applies the three principles of marginal principle, cost-benefit principle and opportunity cost principle.

Demand

Individual demand (consumer demand) is just the demand that an individual has for a given product over an amount of supply.

A reservation price for in demand, is the maximum price that an individual is willing to pay for a specific quantity of a product.

Then, the market demand, all we must do is sum all of the individual demands. If Joe would buy 3 bananas at $4, and Sara would buy 3 bananas at $7, the market demand (just given this information) for 6 bananas would be $11.

Market demand can be moved around by changing incomes, economic conditions and external markets moving (which lessens the free income of individuals).

where PPP represents price and QQQ represents quantity. Essentially, it is the gradient of the curve - and shows the behaviour of the quantity relative to the price.

If the market is elastic, it means that the demand is circumstantial - something like ice cream would have high elasticity. A life-saving medication like insulin would be inelastic; even if the price is pushed higher, people still need insulin.

Supply

We first consider when firms exist in perfect competition - such that:

  • Firms sell identitical goods
  • Firms are price takers.

In supply, reservation price considers the minimum price that a firm would take to produce a specific quantity of product.

Firm supply

Firm supply details what a single firm is willing to supply a good at different prices and quantities.

Market supply

The calculation of market supply is the same as market demand, where we add the quantities of each firm supply to create a new quantitiy - for example if firm X will supply 3 bananas at $2 and firm Y will supply 5 bananas at $5, the market demand is 8 bananas at $7.

Supply can be shifted by input prices (cost of production), a betterment of technology, etc.

An highly elastic market in supply means that for a small change in price, a lot more quantity will be produced; vice versa.

Market equilibrium

Consider the market price is at $2. At $2, suppliers are willing to supply 2 bananas, but consumers want 5. There is excess demand here, and thus there will be positive price pressure.

Conversely, think about if the price is at $4. Suppliers are willing to provide 4 bananas, but consumers only want 2 bananas. So there is negative price pressure.

The equilibrium price is at the intersection of the supply and demand graphs intersect. We now know how many bananas should be farmed, and who will get these bananas.

Often, market supply and demand can change. We denote changes in demand on a graph using apostrophes, D'. We can consider new demand and supply curves to compare with old markets.

Types of competition

Perfect competition

Perfect competition is when there are multiple businesses selling the same product. There are many sellers, and it is easy to enter the market.

As a result, the supply graph is constant - the marginal cost is the same as the marginal profit.

Oligopoly

An oligopoly is formed when there are a few large supplier sin a market. Most markets in the modern world are oligopolies - for example, supermarkets in Australia are dominated by Coles and Woolworths, cola-flavoured drinks are dominated by Coca Cola and Pepsi.

In oligopoly markets, the way your competitors behave have a major impact on you (as there is limited competitors), so your decisions must be based reactionary.

Monopolistic competition

Monopolistic competition is when there is a market with many small businesses competing, each selling differentiated products. The businesses have some power due to the product differentiation.

Consider that if a pizza shop raises it's prices, it won't just lose all of it's customers linearly to the burger shop across the road.

Monopoly

A price-taker firm (or a firm in a perfectly competitive market), is perfectly elastic; as in the quantity supplied will always yield to the same unit price. In simpler terms, a price-taker firm only chooses it's quantity.

A monopoly, however is elastic as it is the only supplier. The more quantity supplied, the less price the firm can charge. In simpler terms, a monopoly chooses both it's price and quantity.

Marginal analysis is important for monopolies - we can draw the marginal cost and marginal revenue plots, and we should only produce up till the point where the marginal cost and marginal revenue equations intersect.

Market efficiency

Consumer surplus

CS=Marginal benefit−Price\text{CS} = \text{Marginal benefit} - \text{Price} CS=Marginal benefit−Price

Consumer surplus is the difference between reservation price and actual price - or more simply; it's the difference between the price the consumer would have been willing to pay and the price they actually paid.

A consumer will participate in the market as long as the paid price is below the reservation price. Graphically, this is the area between the price paid and the demand curve.

Producer surplus

PS=Price−Marginal cost\text{PS} = \text{Price} - \text{Marginal cost} PS=Price−Marginal cost

Producer surplus is the difference between the price the supplier would be willing to supply and the price they actually received.

The producer surplus is the area below the paid price, between the supply curve and the paid price line.

Economic surplus

Economic surplus is then defined by:

Economic surplus=Consumer surplus+Producer surplus\text{Economic surplus} = \text{Consumer surplus} + \text{Producer surplus} Economic surplus=Consumer surplus+Producer surplus

Market failure

Market failure is when overall surplus is not maximised.

Imperfect competition

The following conditions are required for perfect competition:

  • Both buyers and sellers are price takers
  • Sellers produce identitical goods
  • Free entry and exit in the market If any of these conditions are violated, this can cause market failure.

Externalities and imperfect information

The following additional conditions must be satisfied:

  • There are no externalities
  • There is full information

An externality, or some external source of influence from the market is categorised into positive and negative, by virtue of the effect it has on the bystanders of the market.

Private information is information that is exchanged between market participants without the knowledge of other participants, and can cause an inefficiency in the market due to those participants having greater clarity on the economic conditions.

Law

Overview

  • The law is a key consideration when making business decisions. Depending on the scenario, the law can be a constraint (regulations) or an enabler (creation).
  • The law of course dictates how a business may function.
  • However, just because the law permits an action, does not mean it is a good business decision.

From here on out ACL sXX refers to the Australian Consumer Law and it's respective section.

How are laws made?

Sources

Laws come from two main sources:

  1. Parliament: which create statute law and acts of parliament, and:
  2. Courts: which create statutory interpretations and common law.

Statue law/acts of parliament

Commonwealth laws, or federal laws, are made through the follow process:

  • The bill is presented in each house of the governmnent, and discussed 3 times - these are formally called 'readings'.
  • If the bills are approved by both houses, the bill goes to the Governor-General, which upon approval:
  • Is published in the Commonwealth Government Gazette.

State laws have the same process, bu is instead approved and signed by the governor.

Courts

  • Judges make decisions following the legal rule of doctrine of precendence - previous decisions from higher coruts in the same hierarchy should be followed facing similar facts;
    • Essentially, acts/laws cannot cover all scenarios.
    • In light of this, judges in all levels of courts will create precedences by making judgements on cases.
    • These judgements or precedents are now considered common law - and can be referenced in future cases as if it were a law.

Tribunals make judgements about administrative decisions.

Competition Law

Competition law is enforced by government authorties to ensure that markets and businesses operate appropiately. While it is impossible to ensure perfect competition in every market; and in fact doing so may not be in the interests of governments, it is important to reduce monopolisation of sectors.

Anti-competition behavior includes:

  • Cartel conduct (ACL s45): businesses making agreements to fix prices, rig bids, share markets, etc.
  • Misuse of market power: A business with substantial power in the market is now allowed to engage in conduct that substantially lessens competition in the market
  • Anti-competitive contracts, arrangements and understandings: practices that have the purpose, effect or likely effect of substantially lessening competition in a market.
  • Exclusive dealing: attaching anti-competitive terms or conditions to the supply or acquisition of goods or services.
  • Resale price maintenance: requiring customers to sell goods or services at or above a specific minimum price
  • Anti-competitive mergers: mergers that give a single corporation overt market power.

Consumer Law

The Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACC) regulates consumer law.

Australian Consumer Law (ACL) sections

  • Misleading or deceptive conduct s18
  • False or misleading representations s29
  • Unconscionable conduct ss 20 and 21 (more serious than s18)
  • Unfair contract terms s23 - 28A
  • Consumer guarantees ss51 to 62
  • Product safety Part 3-3

Consumer law ensures rights for consumers, including:

  • Unconscionable conduct (ACL s18): businesses must not engage in unconscionable conduct
  • Consumer guarantees: businesses must provide an automatic guarantee to consumers with any product or service, including that they work as advertised.
  • Consumer product safety: the products and services that businesses supply must be safe and and meet consumer guarantees
  • Fair contract terms: businesses need to make sure that their contract terms don't unfairly disadvantage consumers or small businesses
  • Fair sales practices: businesses must conduct fair sales practices

Businesses CANNOT compel their consumers to waive these protections.

Contract Law

Contract enforceability

  • Legal capacity
    • Parties must be legally capable of entering into a partnership. They must understand what they are doing - minors, intoxicated, mental capacity, etc.
  • Consent
    • The parties must genuinely consent, and must not be under duress or unconscionable conduct
  • Legality of purpose
    • The objects or purpose of the contract must be legal
  • Form
    • Contracts can be oral unless required to be in writing by law

What forms a contract?

Contract law revolves around contracts; legally binding agreements between parties. The basics of a contract are:

Agreement

  • An offer is made by one party, promising to do something
  • The other party accepts

Intention

  • There must be an intention to create a legal relationship by both parties

Consideration

  • Consideration for the offer - some payment of some kind for the offer must be made.

Always go through the details a contract in a contract law question.

Important ACL sections

S23 A term of a consumer contract or small business contract is void if:

  1. the term is unfair; and
  2. the contract is a standard form contract

S24 A term of a consumer contract or small business contract is unfair if:

  1. it would cause a significant imbalancein the partie's rights and obligations arising under the contract; and
  2. it is not reasonably necessary in order to protect the legitimate interests of the party who would be advantaged by the term; and
  3. it would cause detriment (whether financial or otherwise) to a party if it were to be applied or relied on.

Corporations law

Corporations law is under the jurisdiction of ASIC (Australian Securities and Investment Commission)

Comrporation law dictates how companies operate and are structured. There are four types of companies that are classified along to the extent of the liability to their members:

  • Companies limited my shares (public or propietary)
  • Companies limited by guarantee (public only)
  • Unlimited companies (public or private)
  • No liability company (public only)

Duties of directors

  • Be honest and careful in their dealings at all times
  • Know what their company is doing financially and practically
  • Ensure that the company can and does pay it's debts on time
  • Ensure that the company keeps proper financial records
  • Act in the company's best interests
  • Ensure that any information received is used properly and in the best interests of the company
  • Disclose any conflicts of interest

Property Law

Property can be divided into three categories:

  • Real property
    • Govered by state and territory law
    • Regulates transactions concerning land and things attached to land
  • Personal property
    • Commonwealth law
    • Personal property includes, eg: vehicles, phones, cameras, etc.
    • Regulated by Personal Property Securities Act 2009
  • Intellectual property
    • Commonwealth law
    • A type of personal property
    • Protects creations of the mind eg: trademarks, copyright, patents

Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)

Overview

  • CSR reflects a company's commitment to operating as a responsible and sustainable business.
  • CSR looks beyond profit and legal compliance to consider how businesses can have a positve impact on society, the environment and the stakeholders of the business.
  • CSR is about how managers can apply ethical principles to make business decisions.

Core characteristics of CSR

  • Voluntary
  • Managing externalities
  • Multiple stakeholder orientation
  • Social and economic alignment
  • Practices and values
  • Beyond philanthropy

Stakeholders

Stakeholders are people who affect and are affected by corporate decisions (this is related to Freeman's stakeholder theory). Stakeholder view/theory is associated with the broader view of corporate social responsibility.

To make good ethical decisions, we must always consider the people who are affected by our decisions. It's important to consider who has the power - and what specific group of stakeholders are affected, and how.

Shareholders v.s stakeholders

A Friedman view of businesses believes that increasing shareholder value is of the utmost importance for a business.

The Freeman view of businesses believes that creating and sustaining value of stakeholders is of utmost importatnce for a business.

Stakeholder subgroups

There are three main group of stakeholders:

  • Organisational: Internal stakeholders, such as employees, managers and investors.
  • Market: Entities who directly engage with the company but are not apart of the company; including competitotrs, consumers and suppliers.
  • Societal: Entities that are causally affected by the company's decisions - the environment, the government, community, the economy, etc.

EXAM TIP: When you are asked about stakeholders at all - always classify what subgroup the stakeholders are in.

Approaches to business decisions

  • The normative approach focuses on what is ethical; impacts on stakeholders, environment, etc.
  • The instrumental approach focuses on business functions; profitability, regulatory compliance, etc.

Carroll's CSR pyramid

  1. Philanthropic responsibilities

This is the top of the pyramid; and thus can be considered the "least" important one. It engages businesses to be charitable to the communities they are engaged in.

  1. Ethical responsibilities

Companies should what is ethically right; beyond being a profitable company - if profits come in the way of ethical ambiguity, then companies should avoice such activities.

  1. Legal responsibilities

At the core - companies should be following the laws that govern the countries they operate in. Without this; companies can come under legal scrutiny which affects the company's ability to engage in the rest of the activities in the pyramid.

  1. Economical responsibilities

The most important one for businesses - without profitability and economic upside, a business simply cannot survive.
